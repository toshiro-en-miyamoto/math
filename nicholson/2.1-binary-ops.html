2.1 Binary Operations

A binary operation ∗ on a set M is a mapping that assigns

  • to each ordered pair ( a , b ) of elements of M
  • an element a ∗ b of M

In this case M is said to be closed under the binary operation.

Abstract algebra is primarily concerned with the study of operations analogous to the addition and multiplication of numbers. The addition process for numbers assigns to any ordered pair ( a , b ) of numbers a new number, their sum, denoted a + b . Similarly, multiplication assigns the product ab a ⁢ b to the pair ( a , b ) .

Theorem 1. If a binary operation has a unity, that unity is unique.

Proof. If e and f are both unities, then f = e ∗ f and e ∗ f = e . So e = f .

Monoid: A set M is called a monoid if a binary operation is defined on M that

  • is associative, and
  • has an unity.

If the operation is commutative, we say that M is a commutative monoid.

We say that ( M , ∗ ) is a monoid if the operation ∗ is to be emphasized.

Convention. In dealing with monoids in general, we use multiplicative notation, and denote the unity by 1.

Multiplicative Notation: Here a ∗ b is written as a ⁢ b (or sometimes a · b ) and is called the product of a and b. The multiplicative unity is denoted 1 (or 1 M if the monoid M must be emphasized).

When discussing monoids in general, we have been using ∗ for the binary operation. But algebraists do not do this. Multiplicative notation is the most popular format among algebraists. Hence we adopt the convention.

Theorem 4. If M is a monoid and a ∈ M has an inverse in M, then that inverse is unique.

Proof. If both b and b′ are inverses of a, then a ⁢ b = 1 = b ⁢ a and a ⁢ b′ = 1 = b′ ⁢ a . Hence b′ = b′ ⁢ 1 = b′ ⁢ ( a ⁢ b ) = ( b′ ⁢ a ) ⁢ b = 1 ⁢ b = b .

Multiplicative inverses. If a is a unit in a multiplicative monoid, the inverse of a is denoted a − 1 .

If the monoid is additive, the inverse of a is denoted − a and is called the negative of a.
