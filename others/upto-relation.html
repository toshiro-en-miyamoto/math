Up to isomorphism

Isomorphic partial orders are explained in [Schumacher 1996] as;

  • Suppose that we had not labeled the elements in the lattice diagrams above. Then we would have been observing only the order structure of the set and nothing else. [...] The differences between them amounts only to a relabeling of the [elements].
  • Two mathematical structures are called isomorphic if they are the same up to a relabeling of the elements involved (the same up to isomorphism).

The fundamental theorem of arithmatic is described as;

  • in [Schumacher 1996]: Every positive integer can be written as a product of prime numbers, and that factorization is unique up to the order of the factors
  • in [Nicholson 2012]: Every integer n ≥ 2 is a product of (one or more) primes. This factorization is unique up to the order of the factors. That is, if n = p 1 ⁢ p 2 ⁢ … ⁢ p r and n = q 1 ⁢ q 2 ⁢ … ⁢ q s , where p i and q j are primes, then r = s and q j can be relabeled so that p i = q i for all i = 1 , 2 , … , r .

[Nicholson 2012] describes the isomorphism as follows:

  • If G and H are groups, a mapping σ : G → H is called an isomorphism if σ is a bijection (one-to-one and onto) which is also a homomorphism.
  • if σ : G → H is an isomorphism, the group H is just G with the change of notation g ↦ σ ⁡ ( g ) .

Sakai shows the theorem in [Sakai 2011] as:

  • すべての自然数 n ≥ 2 は素数の積に分解され, その分解は積の順序を除いて一意的である.
  • 2通りの素因数分解が得られたとする: n = p 1 ⁢ p 2 ⁢ … ⁢ p r = q 1 ⁢ q 2 ⁢ … ⁢ q s , ただし p i , q j はすべて素数とする. このとき r = s であり, 番号を付け直すことにより, p 1 = q 1 , … , p r = q r となる [...]
