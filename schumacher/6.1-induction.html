6.1.5 Theorem (Principle of mathematical induction)

Let Pn be a sequence of statements. Suppose that

  • P1 is true and
  • if Pk is true, then P k + 1 is true
Then Pn is true for all n∈ℕ .

By the second hypothesis in our theorem we know that "if Pk is true, then P k + 1 is true." Therefore P k + 1 is true, and k + 1 ∈S . Then induction axiom then allows us to conclude that S=ℕ . ¹ In an induction argument, when we go from k to k+1 , we assume that the k th statement is true and use this to prove the ( k+1 ) st statement. The assumption that the k th statement is true is called the induction hypothesis.

Well-ordering

A totally ordered set in which every nonempty subset has a least element is said to be well-ordered.

Theorem (Well-ordering of ℕ)

ℕ is well-ordered.

The well-ordering of ℕ is esspecially relevant to us because it is essential to proving many important facts about the natural numbers and the integers. It is a standard proof technique to identify a nonempty set S of those natural numbers that have a certain property. Because we are guranteed that S has a least element l, we can then use the existence of l to achive some purpose. This is useful in two general sorts of circumstances.

  1. The first is straightforward: In an existence proof (one in which we need to show something exists) the thing we are trying to pinpoint may be the smallest of all natural numbers (or integers) that have a particular property, as in the proof of the division algorithm (Theorem 7.2.1).
  2. The second, less obvious, is in proof by contradiction. The trick is to use a supposed least element l of a nonempty S⊆ℕ to construct another natural number l′ ∈ S that is even smaller than l, thus forcing a contradiction.
