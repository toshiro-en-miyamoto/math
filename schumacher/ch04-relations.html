4 Relations and Orderings

4.1 Relations

4.1.3 Definition – Relations

If A and B are sets, then any subset ρ of A×B is called a relation between A and B.

A subset of A×A is called a relation on A.

4.1.4 Example – Relations

On the set ℝ, let ρ be the relation ≤. Then 23∈ρ, since 2≤3 but 43∉ρ.

Remark. If a∈A and b∈B and ρ is a relation between A and B, we usually avoid the cubersome notion ab∈ρ and write a∼b instead. a∼b is read "a is related to b." In fact, in this case we will probably say "let ∼ be a relation," referring to the relation by the symbol associated with it. In some special cases, other symbols are associated with particular relations. For instance, in the case of Example 4.1.4 we ordinarily write a≤b instead of a∼b.

We often consider relations that have conveient special properties. Reflexivity, symmetry, antisymmetry, and transitivity are some of these properties.

4.1.6 Definition – Relation Properties

Let ∼ be a relation on a set A. For xyz ∈A ,

∼is if then
1. reflexive x∼x
2. symmetric x∼y y∼x
3. antisymmetric x∼y ∧ y∼x x=y
4. transitive x∼y ∧ y∼z x∼z

4.2 Orderings

In the previous section we mentioned the order ≤ on ℝ as a familiar example of a relation. Order relations are funcdamental to many branches of mathmatics.

4.2.1 Definition – Partial Orderings

A relation ≤ on a set A is called a partial ordering if ≤ is reflexive, antisymmetric, and transitive. A set with a partial ordering on it is called a partially ordered set.
