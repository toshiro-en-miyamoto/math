4.1 Relations

4.1.3 Definition – Relations

If A and B are sets, then any subset ρ of A×B is called a relation between A and B.

A subset of A×A is called a relation on A.

4.1.4 Example – Relations

On the set ℝ, let ρ be the relation ≤. Then ( 2 , 3 ) ∈ ρ , since 2≤3 but ( 4 , 3 ) ∉ ρ .

Remark. If a∈A and b∈B and ρ is a relation between A and B, we usually avoid the cubersome notion ( a , b ) ∈ ρ and write a∼b instead. a∼b is read "a is related to b." In fact, in this case we will probably say "let ∼ be a relation," referring to the relation by the symbol associated with it. In some special cases, other symbols are associated with particular relations. For instance, in the case of Example 4.1.4 we ordinarily write a≤b instead of a∼b.

We often consider relations that have conveient special properties. Reflexivity, symmetry, antisymmetry, and transitivity are some of these properties.

4.1.6 Definition – Relation Properties

Let ∼ be a relation on a set A. For x , y , z ∈A ,

∼is if then
1. reflexive x∼x
2. symmetric x∼y y∼x
3. antisymmetric ( x∼y ) ∧ ( y∼x ) x=y
4. transitive ( x∼y ) ∧ ( y∼z ) x∼z
